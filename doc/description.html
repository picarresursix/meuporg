1 Purpose

If you never encountered these, you may throw the first stone.

  • Ever started a project, left it aside and took it again weeks/month/years later without understandig what you did?
  • Ever managed a big project on your one, the kind that involves a hundred pages of LaTeX and/or thousands of lines of code?
  • Ever wondered where the hell that awesome info you found on the net was?

These are problems I have faced. I now do keep a track of the links I found using emacs'org-mode but the files where I put these where basically a mess. Furthermore, I didn't have the courage, when writing some file, to go to the org-one, write what I had to do, put a link to the file I was working on, go back to the file I was working on. It's not a big deal but it is the kind of tiny thing which gets in the way and gets annoying.

Meup.org is the solution I came up with. It is a sort of standard in the way I "work"1 and it is based on two things.

  • A good software to manage todo lists and documentation with hyperlinks and that is good to take quick notes in plain-text. I use emacs'org-mode; I heard vimwiki is a nice substitute if you are that kind of person.
  • A simple way to document what I do and what is left to do intended to be as little cumbersome as possible; which implies having tools to automate annoying tasks.

4 Should I use Meup.org?

If you don't work with plain-text files; that is if you work with an office suite such that libreoffice.org, microsoft office or whatever, then no.

If your projects are not scattered on many files and are of very small size, then probably not.

If you work on projects that are spread throughout several weeks/month/years due to their sizes or the amount of time you can spend on them and if the said projects consist in editing text files2
